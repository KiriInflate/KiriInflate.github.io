Array – Partially Connected

How It Works

The Array – Partially Connected KiriInflatable is constructed using a rectangular array of Optimized KiriInflate, in which adjacent columns are only partially connected. This partial connection creates intermediate gaps that provide more room for contraction. The considerable contraction and visible overlaps create a visual effect reminiscent of woven textiles. However, the elastic restoring force is comparatively weaker.

Standard Sample Parameters

  • Material Thickness: 0.07 mm per side
  • Scale: 64.0 mm × 185.0 mm
  • Single Column Width: 57.0 mm
  • Side Slits Length: 32.0 mm
  • Central Slits Length: 11.0 mm
  • Slip Overlap Distance: 15.8 mm
  • Channel Width: 8.0mm
  • Horizontal array number: 3
  • Vertical array number: 4

❗️Please note: In the design tool, the Single Column Width bar, Side Slits Length bar and the Central Slits Length bar jointly determine the resulting Slit Overlap Distance.

Customization Options and Actuation Control

KiriInflate structures offer high programmability of geometric and mechanical properties through customization of slit patterns on 2D surfaces, allowing for rapid formation of stretchable 3D geometries with tailored mechanical behaviors. Key parameters to adjust include:

Scale

As the overall scale increases, the contraction ratio decreases. As the scale increases, the arch height increases slightly at first, and then drops sharply. Larger inflatables generally increase stretchable length and contraction range, while both contraction and tensile forces increase. However, Increasing sizes may see a slight decrease in contraction ratio due to material resistance. Here we assume that the material resistance plays a dominant role.

Key Structure Parameter: Slip Overlap Distance & Number of Arrays

As the Slip Overlap Distance increases, the contraction ratio increases. As the vertical array number increases, the contraction distance increases; whereas the contraction distance remains similar as the horizontal array number increases.

Material Thickness

The contraction ratio shows little change at first as the thickness increases, but then drops significantly beyond a certain point. Theoretically, increasing material thickness reduces the deformation due to higher internal stress. However, it enhances both contraction and tensile forces.
