Tween Curve Surface

How It Works

The Tween Curve Surface KiriInflate is inspired by the directional flow of muscle fibers. The design begins with two parent curves, between which additional curves are evenly interpolated using the “Tween Curve” function in Rhino. This pattern forms a curved surface upon inflation, with the bending direction perpendicular to the orientation of the air channels.

Standard Sample Parameters

  • Material Thickness: 0.07 mm per side
  • Scale: 69 mm × 80 mm
  • Central-axis Channel Width: 7.4 m
  • Slit Spacing: 4.8~5.8 mm

❗️Please note: In the design tool, the Channel Width is adjusted via the Uniform Channel Division bar. Besides, the Slit Spacing bar is used to adjust the Slit Spacing; however, the values do not correspond one-to-one, as they follow a non-linear curve.

Customization Options and Actuation Control

KiriInflate structures offer high programmability of geometric and mechanical properties through customization of slit patterns on 2D surfaces, allowing for rapid formation of stretchable 3D geometries with tailored mechanical behaviors. Key parameters to adjust include:

Scale

As the scale increases, the arch height increases slightly at first, and then drops sharply. Larger inflatables generally increase stretchable length and contraction range, while both contraction and tensile forces increase. However, Increasing sizes may see a slight decrease in contraction ratio due to material resistance. Here we assume that the material resistance plays a dominant role in the largest sample.

Key Structure Parameter: Channel Width

As the channel width decreases, the arch height increases slightly, but the overall change remains minor. However, in practice, the sample with the widest channels exhibits the least bending deformation. This is likely because the wider channels allow for greater inflation, making the difference in arch height appear less significant across varying widths.

Material Thickness

As the thickness increases, the arch height changes only slightly at first, and then drops sharply. We believe this is because, initially, the increased thickness enhances the channel’s pressure-bearing capacity, leading to greater deformation under higher pressure. However, as the material becomes thicker, the internal stress increases, which ultimately limits further deformation.
