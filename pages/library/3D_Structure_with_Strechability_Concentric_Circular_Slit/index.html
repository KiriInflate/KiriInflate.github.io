Concentric Circular Slit

How It Works

The Concentric Circular Slit KiriInflate can be understood as a ring-shaped version of the Array – Fully Connected KiriInflate, where the pattern is wrapped along slits into a circular configuration. Its defining feature is a series of concentric circular slits whose lengths gradually increase from the center to the outer edge. This pattern generates a saddle-like geometry upon inflation, characterized by high elasticity and negative Gaussian curvature. This configuration is particularly well-suited for applications requiring multi-directional surface adaptability—such as soft robotic skins, adaptive seating systems, or wearable exoskeletal supports for complex anatomical regions.

Customization Options and Actuation Control

KiriInflate structures offer high programmability of geometric and mechanical properties through customization of slit patterns on 2D surfaces, allowing for rapid formation of stretchable 3D geometries with tailored mechanical behaviors. Key parameters to adjust include:

Scale

As the scale increases, the contraction decreases, while the arch height changes slightly. Larger inflatables generally increase stretchable length and contraction range, while both contraction and tensile forces increase. However, Increasing sizes may see a slight decrease in contraction ratio due to material resistance. Here we assume that the material resistance plays a dominant role.

Key Structure Parameter: Inter-slit Angle

As the inter-slit angle decreases, the contraction ratio changes slightly, while the arch height increases progressively. Decreasing the inter-slit angle increases the overlap distance between slits. However, this change has little impact on the contraction ratio, especially in cases where the slits are less likely to overlap on curved surfaces.

Material Thickness

As the thickness increases, both the contraction ratio and the arch height decrease progressively. Theoretically, increasing material thickness reduces the deformation due to higher internal stress. However, it enhances both contraction and tensile forces.
